Buried Treasure

Britain's Greatest Ancient Coin Hoards ‚Äî and the stories of why they were never reclaimed

Britain has yielded more than 1,200 documented Roman coin hoards ‚Äî more per square mile than anywhere else in the former Roman Empire. From the gold and silver of fleeing Romano-British families to the ritual offerings of Iron Age priests, these buried treasures tell stories of crisis, faith, and the fragility of civilisation.

Why so many hoards? Britain's position at the edge of empire made it vulnerable. When central authority collapsed, there was no one to return to. The coins stayed in the ground ‚Äî waiting for metal detectorists, farmers, and archaeologists to find them fifteen centuries later.

The largest hoard of late Roman gold and silver found anywhere in the former Roman Empire. Discovered by Eric Lawes, who was using a metal detector to find a lost hammer for his friend ‚Äî he found the hammer too, which is now also in the British Museum.

The hoard was buried after AD 407 in an oak chest, carefully organised with items wrapped in cloth and stored in smaller boxes. The owners clearly intended to return. They never did.

Contents include 569 gold solidi, 14,212 siliquae, 29 pieces of gold jewellery, 98 silver spoons, and remarkable objects like the famous "Hoxne Tigress" handle and the Empress pepper pot. The gold alone weighs 3.5 kg; the silver 23.75 kg.

Notable Objects

  • The "Empress" pepper pot (pipiratorium) ‚Äî finest surviving example
  • Gold body-chain ‚Äî extremely rare survival
  • Silver tigress handle ‚Äî masterwork of late Roman art
  • Inscribed spoons naming family members (Aurelius Ursicinus)
  • Peter Whatling's hammer

The largest coin hoard ever found in Britain by raw count. Discovered by metal detectorists Peter Humphries and John Booth on Crown Estate land near the Roman town of Cunetio.

Unlike Hoxne's precious metals, Cunetio contains mostly debased radiates (antoniniani) ‚Äî the everyday currency of the Crisis of the Third Century. This was someone's life savings at a time when inflation was destroying the Roman economy.

Many coins are from the breakaway Gallic Empire of Postumus, showing how Britain was part of this separatist realm during the chaos of the 260s-270s.

Historical Context

  • Buried during the Crisis of the Third Century
  • Coins mostly minted under Gallic Empire emperors
  • Site was an important Romano-British market town
  • Featured on Time Team in 2009

The Frome Hoard contains the largest group of Carausius coins ever found ‚Äî 766 pieces from Britain's breakaway emperor who ruled from 286-293 AD. Five of these are silver denarii bearing the mysterious "RSR" inscription (Virgil's golden age poetry).

The coins were so heavy (160 kg) that the pot would have needed to be buried first, then filled ‚Äî no one could have carried it.

The largest hoard of British Iron Age coins ever found. Discovered by local volunteers of the Hallaton Fieldwork Group, this treasure more than doubled the known number of Corieltauvian coins.

This wasn't hidden wealth ‚Äî it was a ritual deposit at an open-air shrine. The site includes pig bones (offerings), a sacrificed dog skeleton believed to guard the treasure, and a remarkable silver-gilt Roman parade helmet ‚Äî one of only three known from Britain.

The hoard includes the oldest Roman coin found in Britain: a silver piece dated to 211 BC, evidence of pre-conquest trade or diplomacy with Rome.

Ritual Objects

  • Silver-gilt Roman parade helmet (Hallaton Helmet)
  • Sacrificed dog skeleton ‚Äî ritual guardian
  • Silver ring inscribed "TOT" (god Toutatis)
  • Pig bones from ritual feasting
  • 4,835 Corieltauvi tribal coins

A series of spectacular finds over 44 years, including the famous Great Torc ‚Äî perhaps the finest piece of Celtic metalwork ever found. The quality suggests royal ownership, possibly belonging to the Iceni kings who would later produce Boudica.

üìú Why Were So Many Hoards Buried in Britain?

Crisis of the Third Century

AD 235-284 brought civil wars, plague, and economic collapse. Wealthy Romano-Britons buried their savings as raiders threatened.

End of Roman Britain

After AD 410, there was no Roman authority to return to. Families fled to Gaul or died in the chaos ‚Äî their treasure stayed buried.

Boudica's Revolt (AD 60-61)

The Iceni rebellion destroyed three cities. Romans and Britons alike hid valuables during the violence.

Celtic Ritual Practice

Iron Age Britons deposited valuables as votive offerings ‚Äî gifts to gods at sacred sites, never meant to be retrieved.

Banks Don't Exist

No safe deposit boxes in the ancient world. The ground was the safest vault ‚Äî if you survived to dig it up.

Island at Empire's Edge

Britain's distance from Rome made it vulnerable when central authority weakened. No legions to protect returning owners.

"The very act of not returning for a buried hoard is itself evidence of catastrophe ‚Äî whether personal death, family extinction, or civilisational collapse. Every hoard is a tragedy." ‚Äî Richard Abdy, British Museum, on Romano-British hoards
