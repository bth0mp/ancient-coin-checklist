History of the Mint

c. 286-287 AD
Carausius Establishes the Mint
The usurper Carausius, commander of the Classis Britannica, establishes the Londinium mint after declaring himself emperor of Britain and northern Gaul. His coins feature remarkable literary references, including abbreviated quotes from Virgil's Fourth Eclogue.
293 AD
Allectus Takes Power
Allectus, Carausius's finance minister, assassinates his emperor and takes control. The London mint continues operating, though coin quality and artistic style decline. His quinarii with galley reverses are particularly distinctive.
296 AD
Constantius I Reconquers Britain
Caesar Constantius Chlorus defeats Allectus and reintegrates Britain into the Roman Empire. The famous "LON" mintmark medallion celebrates his arrival with the legend "REDDITOR LVCIS AETERNAE" (Restorer of Eternal Light). The mint is reorganized under tetrarchic control.
296-310 AD
Tetrarchic Period
The mint produces coins for all four tetrarchs: Diocletian, Maximian, Constantius I, and Galerius. Mintmarks evolve from LON to variations including PLN (Pecunia Londinium) and MSL.
310-314 AD
Constantine I and Licinius
Following Constantius I's death at York in 306, his son Constantine uses the London mint to assert legitimacy. Coins for both Constantine and his co-emperor Licinius are produced. The mint uses PLN and PLON mintmarks during this period.
c. 314 AD
First Closure
The London mint closes as Constantine consolidates power and reduces the number of provincial mints. Britain would rely on continental mints for coinage.
c. 383 AD
Magnus Maximus Revival (Possible)
Some numismatists attribute certain coins of the usurper Magnus Maximus to a briefly reopened London mint (mintmark AVG or AVGOB). This attribution remains debated.

Identification Tips

üé® Style Recognition

London coins have a distinctive style ‚Äî portraits tend to be less refined than continental mints but have character. Carausius coins often show a distinctive "heavy-jawed" portrait style. Compare with known examples before attributing.

‚öñÔ∏è Weight Standards

  • Antoninianus (Carausius): ~4g average, often lighter than official issues
  • Quinarius (Allectus): ~2.5-3g, distinctive galley reverses
  • Follis (Tetrarchic): ~10g initially, decreasing over time
  • Nummus (Constantine): ~3-4g reduced follis

üè∫ Patina & Metal

British finds often show distinctive green/brown patinas from UK soil conditions. Genuine London coins may have slightly different alloy compositions than continental issues ‚Äî Carausius in particular used variable silver content.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Fakes

Carausius coins are frequently forged due to high collector demand. Watch for:

  • Too-perfect legends (ancient dies had imperfections)
  • Wrong weight or flan shape
  • Modern tool marks visible under magnification
  • Suspiciously good condition for the type
