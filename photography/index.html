üì∏ Why Photograph Your Collection?

Good photography serves your collection in multiple ways:

  • Insurance documentation ‚Äî Essential for claims. Photograph every coin with a ruler for scale.
  • Before/after conservation ‚Äî Track changes and preserve the "as-received" state.
  • Sharing & selling ‚Äî Quality photos are crucial for forums, sales, and publications.
  • Personal archive ‚Äî Build a catalog that outlasts the physical collection.
  • Research & attribution ‚Äî High-quality images help with die studies and RIC matching.

üí° Tip: Photograph on Arrival

Take photos of every coin when it arrives, before any handling or storage. This creates a permanent record of the coin's condition at acquisition ‚Äî invaluable for insurance and future reference.

üõ†Ô∏è Equipment Overview

Camera Options

üì± Budget: Smartphone $0 (use what you have)

Modern smartphone cameras are surprisingly capable. The iPhone 13+ and recent Android flagships can capture excellent coin photos with proper lighting. Main limitation: macro capability and manual controls.

  • Use the telephoto lens if available (2x-3x optical zoom)
  • Consider a clip-on macro lens ($10-30)
  • Lock focus and exposure before shooting

üì∑ Mid-Range: Compact Camera or Mirrorless $300-800

Cameras like the Fuji X100V, Sony RX100, or entry-level mirrorless bodies offer excellent quality with macro modes. The fixed-lens compacts are portable; mirrorless allows lens changes.

  • Fuji X100V: 23mm f/2, excellent macro mode down to 10cm
  • Sony RX100 VII: 24-200mm zoom with close focus
  • Canon M50/Sony A6000 series with macro lens

üéØ Professional: DSLR/Mirrorless + Macro Lens $1,000+

A dedicated macro lens (100mm f/2.8 or similar) with 1:1 reproduction ratio delivers the best results. Working distance allows proper lighting without shadows.

  • Canon 100mm f/2.8L Macro IS
  • Sony 90mm f/2.8 Macro G OSS
  • Laowa 100mm f/2.8 2x Ultra Macro (budget macro option)

Essential Accessories

  • Tripod or copy stand ‚Äî Camera shake ruins macro shots. A basic tabletop tripod ($20-40) or copy stand ($50-100) is essential.
  • Remote shutter or self-timer ‚Äî Even pressing the shutter button can cause blur. Use 2-second timer at minimum.
  • Lighting ‚Äî See detailed section below. This is where most improvement happens.
  • Neutral backgrounds ‚Äî Gray, black, or white cards/fabric. Avoid busy patterns.

üí° Lighting Techniques

Lighting is the single most important factor in coin photography. A cheap camera with great lighting will outperform an expensive camera with poor lighting every time.

The Three Main Approaches

1. Diffused Daylight (Free)

Natural light from a north-facing window (or overcast sky) provides soft, even illumination. Position the coin near the window but not in direct sunlight.

  • Pros: Free, natural color, soft shadows
  • Cons: Inconsistent, limited to daytime, weather-dependent
  • Best for: Quick photos, bronze patina, testing setups

2. Angled Lights (Side Lighting)

Two adjustable desk lamps at 45¬∞ angles from opposite sides. This creates dimension and highlights relief.

3. Pseudo-Axial / Ring Light

Light comes from directly above/around the lens, illuminating the coin perpendicular to its surface. Creates even lighting with minimal shadows.

üîß Recommended Ring Lights

Laowa Front LED Ring Light ‚Äî Powerful, designed for macro, battery-powered option. ~$100

Amazon basics ring light ‚Äî Works for phones and small cameras. ~$20-40

DIY option: LED strip around a cardboard tube that fits your lens.

  • Pros: Even lighting, shows toning/luster, no shadows
  • Cons: Can look "flat," may miss some surface detail
  • Best for: Shiny silver, toned coins, consistent catalog photos

Lighting by Metal

Metal Challenge Solution
Silver Bright reflections, toning Ring light or heavily diffused side lights. Gray background.
Gold Extreme reflectivity Diffuse everything. White background often works best.
Bronze/AE Dark surfaces, patina Angled lights to catch patina color. May need longer exposure.
Billon Mixed surface texture Combination: ring light + one angled fill.

üí° The Diffusion Secret

Raw light from an LED or bulb is harsh. Soften it with:

  • White tissue paper taped over the light
  • Translucent plastic containers
  • Professional diffusion panels ($10-30)
  • Bouncing light off white paper/foam board

The Key Settings

Aperture f/8 to f/11 Sweet spot for sharpness and depth of field. f/2.8 = razor-thin focus. f/16+ = diffraction softness.

Depth of Field in Macro

At close focusing distances, depth of field becomes extremely shallow. Even at f/11, you may only have 1-2mm in focus. For high-relief coins:

  • Focus on the eyes (for portraits) ‚Äî viewers look there first
  • Stop down to f/11-f/16 ‚Äî accept some diffraction for more DOF
  • Focus stacking (advanced) ‚Äî Take multiple shots at different focus points and blend in software

üé¨ Setup & Workflow

Step-by-Step Process

1

Prepare the Space

Find a stable surface away from vibrations (not on a washing machine!). Set up your tripod/copy stand. Ensure consistent ambient light ‚Äî close blinds if daylight varies.

2

Position the Coin

Place on your background. Use a small piece of poster putty underneath to prevent rolling and allow angle adjustment. The coin should be perfectly flat to the camera sensor (parallel planes).

3

Set Up Lighting

Position your lights. Turn off room lights to avoid mixed color temperatures. Take test shots and adjust. Look for even illumination and pleasing shadows.

4

Focus Precisely

Use live view zoom (usually 10x) to focus on the highest relief area. For portraits, focus on the eye or forehead. Use manual focus for consistency.

5

Shoot Both Sides

Take multiple exposures of each side. Bracket exposure if unsure (slightly over/under). Flip the coin by rotating around the vertical axis (like turning a page) to maintain orientation.

6

Include Scale Reference

For at least one shot, include a ruler or coin sizing reference. This is essential for documentation and helps others gauge the coin's size.

‚ö†Ô∏è Handling Precautions

Always handle coins by the edges. Wear cotton gloves for valuable pieces. Clean the background between coins to avoid dust transfer. Never touch the coin's surfaces ‚Äî skin oils cause long-term damage.

üé® Backgrounds & Presentation

Background Selection

Background Best For Notes
18% Gray Silver, mixed lots Industry standard. Neutral, doesn't influence toning perception.
Black Silver, bronze Dramatic. Hides edges if coin is dark. Use matte black (felt/velvet).
White Gold, bright silver Clean, auction-style. Can blow out highlights on shiny coins.
Blue Gold specifically Traditional for gold photography. Provides contrast.

üí° DIY Backgrounds

  • Gray card: $5-10 from any camera store
  • Black velvet: Craft store fabric, absorbs light beautifully
  • Construction paper: Works in a pinch, replace when worn
  • Poster board: Smooth, cheap, replaceable

Framing & Composition

  • Fill the frame ‚Äî The coin should occupy 70-80% of the image
  • Consistent orientation ‚Äî Portrait up (12 o'clock at top) unless studying die axis
  • Leave breathing room ‚Äî Small margin around the coin for cropping flexibility
  • Match obverse/reverse ‚Äî Keep both sides at same scale and lighting

üñ•Ô∏è Post-Processing

Recommended Software

  • Adobe Lightroom ‚Äî Industry standard, excellent batch processing
  • Capture One ‚Äî Superior color science, steeper learning curve
  • Darktable ‚Äî Free, open-source Lightroom alternative
  • Photoshop ‚Äî Advanced retouching, focus stacking
  • Snapseed ‚Äî Excellent free mobile editor

Basic Adjustments

  1. White balance ‚Äî Correct any color cast. The background should be neutral.
  2. Exposure ‚Äî Adjust so details are visible in both highlights and shadows.
  3. Contrast ‚Äî Slight increase helps definition. Don't overdo it.
  4. Sharpening ‚Äî Apply carefully. Over-sharpening creates ugly halos.
  5. Crop & straighten ‚Äî Square crop, perfectly level coin.

‚úÖ Good Practice

  • Subtle adjustments
  • Accurate colors
  • Visible surface detail
  • Consistent across collection
  • Preserves original character

‚ùå Avoid

  • Over-saturation
  • Extreme sharpening halos
  • Cloning out flaws (misrepresentation)
  • HDR "grunge" effects
  • Heavy vignetting

‚ö†Ô∏è Ethics in Coin Photography

For sales and attribution, your photos should represent the coin accurately. Removing scratches, enhancing details, or manipulating colors crosses into misrepresentation. Save artistic edits for personal display only.

üö´ Common Mistakes

Technical Errors

  • Camera shake ‚Äî Use tripod + timer. Even small movements ruin macro shots.
  • Wrong focus point ‚Äî Focus on the highest relief, not the flat field.
  • Too wide aperture ‚Äî f/2.8 looks good in portraits, not coins. Use f/8-11.
  • Mixed lighting ‚Äî Room lights + desk lamp = color cast nightmare.
  • JPEG compression ‚Äî Always shoot RAW, or at least highest-quality JPEG.

Lighting Errors

  • Hot spots ‚Äî Bright reflections from undiffused light. Diffuse or angle away.
  • Flat lighting ‚Äî All ring light with no dimension. Add subtle side fill.
  • Dark shadows ‚Äî Single light source creates heavy shadows. Use fill or reflector.
  • Inconsistent color ‚Äî Different bulb types mix poorly. Stick to one light source type.

Setup Errors

  • Coin not level ‚Äî Creates uneven focus. Use putty to adjust angle.
  • Dirty background ‚Äî Dust and fibers show in macro. Clean between shots.
  • Fingerprints on coin ‚Äî Handle by edges only. Visible in oblique lighting.
  • Camera not parallel ‚Äî Creates keystoning (coin looks oval). Level the camera.

üì± Phone Photography Tips

Modern smartphones can take excellent coin photos with the right technique:

Essential Tips

  1. Use the 2x or 3x telephoto lens ‚Äî More working distance, less distortion
  2. Lock focus and exposure ‚Äî Tap and hold on the coin until the lock indicator appears
  3. Prop your phone ‚Äî Use a phone tripod or stack of books for stability
  4. Add a clip-on macro lens ‚Äî $15-30 for dramatic close-up improvement
  5. Use the timer ‚Äî 3-second delay eliminates touch shake

Recommended Phone Apps

  • ProCamera / Halide (iOS) ‚Äî Manual controls, RAW capture
  • Open Camera (Android) ‚Äî Free, full manual control
  • Snapseed ‚Äî Excellent free editor for both platforms

üí° Quick Phone Setup

Lean your phone against something stable (mug, box) pointing down at a 45¬∞ angle. Place the coin on white paper near a window. Use a piece of white paper on the opposite side as a reflector. Tap to focus on the coin, tap the sun icon to reduce exposure slightly. Use 3-second timer. Simple, free, effective.

üíæ Archiving Your Images

File Organization

Develop a consistent naming convention:

Category_Ruler_Denomination_Reference_Side.jpg

Examples:

  • Londinium_Carausius_Antoninianus_RIC-98_Obv.jpg
  • Athens_Classical_Tetradrachm_Starr-group-V_Rev.jpg
  • CelticBritain_Cunobelin_Stater_ABC-2774_Obv.jpg

Backup Strategy

  • Local drive ‚Äî Primary storage on your computer
  • External drive ‚Äî Regular backups, stored separately
  • Cloud storage ‚Äî Google Drive, Dropbox, or iCloud for offsite backup

üí° The 3-2-1 Rule

3 copies of your data, on 2 different types of media, with 1 offsite. For a valuable collection archive, this isn't paranoia ‚Äî it's prudence.

Resolution & Format

  • Archive: Full-resolution RAW or TIFF files
  • Working copies: High-quality JPEG (90%+)
  • Web sharing: 1500-2000px on long edge, JPEG 80%
  • Thumbnails: 400-600px for catalogs and quick reference
