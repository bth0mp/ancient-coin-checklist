1. Types of Forgeries

Understanding how fakes are made helps you spot them. Different production methods leave different telltale signs.

Cast Forgeries Easier to Detect

Made by creating a mold from a genuine coin and pouring molten metal. Most common type.

  • Porous, "bubbly" surface texture
  • Soft, mushy details
  • Seam lines from mold halves
  • Often slightly underweight (shrinkage)
  • File marks where seams were removed

Struck Forgeries Hardest to Detect

Made with hand-cut dies, mimicking ancient production. The most dangerous fakes.

  • Sharp, fresh details
  • May have die polish lines
  • Modern tool marks on edges
  • Often "too perfect" for the type
  • Die axis may be wrong for mint

Electrotype Moderate Risk

Copper shells electroplated from genuine coins, filled with lead or solder.

  • Seam visible on edge
  • Wrong weight (usually light)
  • Hollow sound when dropped
  • Surface may be too "shiny"
  • 19th-century museum copies common

Tooled/Altered Very Deceptive

Genuine coins modified to increase value ‚Äî different emperor, added features, etc.

  • Inconsistent patina in reworked areas
  • Portrait doesn't match legend style
  • Scratches filled and re-toned
  • Metal flow inconsistent
  • May start as a genuine coin

"The best defense against forgeries is to know your coins. Study die links, style, and fabric until authentic coins look 'right' and fakes look 'wrong' ‚Äî even before you can articulate why."

‚Äî Wayne Sayles, Classical Numismatic Group

2. Visual Detection Methods

Surface Examination

‚úì Signs of Authenticity

  • Flow lines: Radiating lines from metal flowing under die pressure
  • Die wear progression: Consistent with known die sequences
  • Natural patina: Develops in recesses, thins on high points
  • Edge characteristics: Rough, irregular from ancient striking
  • Crystalline structure: Visible under magnification in silver

‚úó Warning Signs

  • Casting bubbles: Tiny pits, especially in protected areas
  • Seam lines: Faint lines around the edge circumference
  • Mushy details: Soft, poorly defined features throughout
  • Artificial patina: Too uniform, wrong color, sits on surface
  • Modern tool marks: Machine-perfect edges, lathe marks
  • "Fresh" surfaces: No wear but heavy "patina" ‚Äî contradictory

Style Analysis

This is where experience matters most. Ancient die engravers had distinctive styles, regional variations, and period-appropriate conventions.

Element What to Check Common Forger Mistakes
Lettering Form, spacing, period-appropriate style Modern letter shapes, too uniform spacing
Portrait Hair rendering, facial proportions, neck truncation Wrong portrait style for mint/period, lifeless expression
Reverse Figures Anatomy, drapery folds, attribute details Stiff posture, incorrect attributes, flat drapery
Exergue Line Straight, curved, or absent per type Added where shouldn't exist, wrong style
Border Dotted, linear, or beaded per period Wrong border type for denomination/era

The "Wrong" Test

With experience, authentic coins develop a familiar "feel." When examining a suspect coin, ask:

‚ö° Trust Your Instincts

  • Does anything look "wrong" even if you can't explain it?
  • Is it "too good to be true" ‚Äî perfect centering, full legends, choice surfaces?
  • Does the style match published examples from this mint?
  • Would you stake your reputation on this coin?

If something feels off, investigate further or walk away. Your instincts are often processing information your conscious mind hasn't articulated yet.

3. Physical Testing

Essential Measurements

Test Equipment What It Reveals
Weight Digital scale (0.01g precision) Cast fakes often 5-15% light due to shrinkage; plated fakes may be heavy
Diameter Calipers Cast copies shrink ~2-5%; should match known standards
Die Axis Visual rotation Mints had characteristic axes (Rome: 6 o'clock; Greek: varies)
Specific Gravity Scale + water displacement Gold: 19.3, Silver: 10.5, Bronze: 8.9 ‚Äî forgeries often wrong

Specific Gravity Testing

One of the most reliable physical tests. Measures density by comparing weight in air vs. water:

SG Formula

SG = Weight in Air √∑ (Weight in Air ‚àí Weight in Water)

Expected values:

  • Pure gold: 19.3
  • Electrum: 12-16 (varies by composition)
  • Pure silver: 10.5
  • Ancient silver (debased): 9.5-10.5
  • Bronze: 8.7-8.9
  • Lead (used in cast fakes): 11.3

Note: Fourr√©es (ancient plated coins) and modern plated fakes will show wrong SG.

Non-Destructive Tests

‚úì Safe Tests

  • Magnet test: Ancient silver/gold/bronze are non-magnetic. Any magnetic response = fake or modern
  • Ring test: Silver coins ring when dropped on hard surface; lead-filled fakes thud
  • Ice test (gold): Gold conducts heat rapidly ‚Äî ice melts faster on gold than on fakes
  • Breath test: Authentic patina doesn't change when breathed on; artificial patina may fog differently

‚úó Tests to Avoid

  • Acid testing: Destroys surfaces, voids guarantees
  • Filing/cutting: Permanent damage
  • Aggressive cleaning: Removes patina, lowers value

4. Provenance Red Flags

Where a coin comes from often matters as much as how it looks.

üö© High-Risk Scenarios

  • "Old collection" with no documentation ‚Äî The most common forger story
  • Eastern European or Bulgarian origin ‚Äî Major forgery production centers
  • Seller refuses return policy ‚Äî Legitimate dealers offer guarantees
  • Price too good to be true ‚Äî Rare types at common prices = likely fake
  • No professional photos ‚Äî Hides details that would reveal forgery
  • Auction house you've never heard of ‚Äî Research reputation first
  • Facebook Marketplace / random eBay sellers ‚Äî Buyer beware territory
  • Offered privately at a coin show ‚Äî May be avoiding dealer scrutiny

‚úì Confidence Builders

  • Published provenance ‚Äî Appeared in prior auction catalogs
  • Old collection tickets/envelopes ‚Äî Especially pre-1970
  • Reputable auction house ‚Äî CNG, Roma, NAC, Heritage, etc.
  • Lifetime guarantee of authenticity ‚Äî Standard from major dealers
  • Third-party grading ‚Äî NGC Ancients provides additional review
  • Die match to published specimen ‚Äî Proves same dies used

The Bulgarian Connection

Bulgaria has become the world's primary source of ancient coin forgeries. Skilled craftsmen produce everything from crude tourist fakes to dangerous high-quality struck forgeries.

‚ö†Ô∏è Known Forgery "Families"

Certain prolific forgers' products are now documented:

  • "Slavey" group: Greek silver, especially Macedonian
  • "Lipanoff" workshop: Roman aurei and rare denarii
  • "Becker" (historical): 19th-century dies still producing?

Check databases like Forum Ancient Coins' fake reports before buying.

7. Case Studies

Case 1: The "Perfect" Aureus

Scenario: A Nero aureus offered privately at a major coin show. "Old European collection." Price: $8,000 (market: $15,000+).

Red flags found:

  • Weight: 7.1g (should be ~7.3g for period)
  • Surface showed faint casting porosity under 20x magnification
  • Lettering style didn't match Rome mint products
  • Seller refused to allow specific gravity test

Verdict: Cast forgery. Seller disappeared from the show.

Case 2: The Tooled Portrait

Scenario: Julius Caesar denarius, lifetime issue, excellent portrait. Major auction house, estimated $12,000.

Investigation:

  • Coin was genuine base ‚Äî right weight, style, fabric
  • Portrait showed tooling marks under magnification
  • Original coin likely a lower-grade specimen
  • Hair curls had been "enhanced" with modern tools
  • Artificial toning applied over tooled areas

Verdict: Genuine coin, but tooled to increase grade. Withdrawn from auction after expert review.

Case 3: The Die Link Victory

Scenario: Suspiciously nice Syracuse decadrachm offered online. Seller unknown, price reasonable.

Verification:

  • Photographed coin at multiple angles
  • Compared to published die study (Tudeer)
  • Found exact obverse die match to known genuine specimen
  • Weight matched within 0.1g of die-linked coin
  • Specific gravity: 10.48 (pure silver)

Verdict: Authentic. Purchased and later confirmed by NGC Ancients with XRF.
