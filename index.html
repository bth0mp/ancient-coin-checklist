The birthplace of coinage - organized by city-state and mint

Greek coinage began around 650-600 BC in Lydia and Ionia (modern Turkey) and spread throughout the Mediterranean. Each city-state (polis) minted its own distinctive coins featuring patron deities, mythological creatures, or local symbols. These coins tell the story of Greek civilization - from the rise of democracy in Athens to Alexander's conquest of Persia.

Identifying Features: Roma head in winged helmet (obverse). ROMA legend. Dioscuri riding horses (reverse). X mark = 10 asses.

âš”ï¸ Marc Antony Legionary Denarii (32-31 BC)

The Final Roman Civil War. In 32-31 BC, the Roman world was split between two men: Octavian (the future Augustus) in the West, and Marcus Antonius (Mark Antony) with Cleopatra VII of Egypt in the East. War was inevitable.

To pay his massive army and navy, Antony struck millions of silver denarii at his mobile military mint (probably Patrae in Greece). Each coin named one of his 23+ legions - a roll call of the forces that would decide Rome's fate.

âš”ï¸ MARCUS ANTONIUS (83-30 BC) - Caesar's Heir Who Lost It All

Full Name: Marcus Antonius (Mark Antony)

The Eastern Command: Antony took the wealthy East as his domain. He met Cleopatra at Tarsus in 41 BC and began a legendary romance. He had children with her, gave Roman territories to their children, and effectively made Alexandria his capital - scandalizing Rome.

The Break: By 32 BC, the Triumvirate was over. Octavian read Antony's will to the Senate (illegally obtained), which revealed he wanted to be buried in Alexandria. Rome declared war - not on Antony, but on Cleopatra. The stage was set.

ğŸ¯ The Coin Design

Obverse: War galley (praetorian galley) with ANT AVG III VIR R P C (Antonius Augur Triumvir Rei Publicae Constituendae - "Antony, Augur, Triumvir for Restoring the Republic")

Metal: Silver, but notably debased (~80-90% silver vs. normal ~95%). This lower silver content meant they stayed in circulation for 200+ years as "bad money drives out good" (Gresham's Law).

Weight: ~3.5-3.8g (slightly light)

âš”ï¸ The Battle of Actium (2 September 31 BC)

The decisive naval battle took place off the coast of Greece. Antony and Cleopatra had ~500 ships and 70,000 infantry. Octavian's admiral Agrippa had ~400 ships but better tactics.

The battle was a disaster. Cleopatra's squadron fled through the lines mid-battle - and Antony abandoned his fleet to follow her. His army surrendered. Within a year, both were dead by suicide in Alexandria. Rome became an Empire.

These coins paid the soldiers who fought that day.

ğŸ’° Why Are These Coins So Common Today?

Mark Antony's legionary denarii are among the most common Roman silver coins because:

  • Massive mintage: Millions struck to pay 23+ legions
  • Debased silver: At ~80-90% pure, they were worth less than other denarii, so people hoarded the good coins and spent these
  • Gresham's Law: They circulated for 200+ years until Trajan finally withdrew them
  • Many hoards: Found across the Roman world in large numbers

Despite being common, they remain one of the most historically significant Roman coins - direct witnesses to the end of the Republic.
