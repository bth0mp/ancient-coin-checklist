The Poet Emperor

How Carausius quoted Virgil on British coins ‚Äî and what it tells us about literacy at the edge of empire

The Mystery Letters

For over two centuries, scholars puzzled over strange letter sequences on Carausius's coins. The marks RSR and INPCDA appeared on silver denarii and bronze medallions from Roman Britain, but no one could explain them.

Then in 1998, numismatist Guy de la B√©doy√®re cracked the code. The letters were abbreviations ‚Äî quotations from Virgil's poetry, struck into metal at the edge of the Roman world.

Virgil's Fourth Eclogue

Virgil wrote his Fourth Eclogue around 40 BC, a messianic poem prophesying the return of a Golden Age. The relevant lines:

Ultima Cumaei venit iam carminis aetas;
magnus ab integro saeclorum nascitur ordo.
Iam redit et Virgo, redeunt Saturnia regna,
iam nova progenies caelo demittitur alto.
"Now comes the final age of the Cumaean song;
the great order of the ages is born anew.
Now the Virgin returns, the reign of Saturn returns,
now a new generation descends from high heaven."

Carausius abbreviated these lines on his coins:

Mark Full Latin Translation
RSR Redeunt Saturnia Regna "The Golden Age Returns"
INPCDA Iam Nova Progenies Caelo Demittitur Alto "Now a new generation descends from high heaven"

A Third Virgilian Reference

Carausius didn't stop with the Eclogues. Some of his silver coins bear the legend EXPECTATE VENI ‚Äî "Come, long-awaited one."

This echoes Aeneid Book II, line 283, where the ghost of Hector appears to Aeneas with the words "expectate venis?" ‚Äî "Have you come, the long-awaited one?"

Carausius was positioning himself as a prophesied, heaven-sent savior ‚Äî not a usurper, but a restorer of Rome's greatness.

Why This Matters

A naval commander from the Menapii tribe (modern Belgium/Netherlands), Carausius was dismissed by Roman historians as a pirate and usurper. But his coinage tells a different story:

  1. Classical Education: Using Virgilian abbreviations required that both Carausius and his audience understood the references. This suggests a surprisingly literate elite in late 3rd-century Britain.
  2. Sophisticated Propaganda: Rather than brute force claims to power, Carausius appealed to Rome's own cultural heritage. He wasn't rejecting Rome ‚Äî he was claiming to restore it.
  3. Messianic Legitimacy: The Fourth Eclogue was already famous for its prophetic quality. (Christians later read it as predicting Christ.) Carausius used this association to frame his rule as divinely ordained.
  4. Poetry on Currency: These may be the only ancient coins to quote directly from classical literature in abbreviated form. It's remarkably unusual ‚Äî and remarkably sophisticated.

The Famous Medallion

The INPCDA abbreviation appears on a rare bronze medallion now in the British Museum (accession 1967,9-1,1). The reverse shows Victory in a chariot with the legend VICTORIA CARAVSI AVG ‚Äî "Victory of the Emperor Carausius."

The RSR mark is far more common, appearing in the exergue (bottom field) of most Carausius silver denarii. These were substantial, good-quality silver coins that contrasted sharply with the debased antoniniani of mainland Rome.

  • RSR appears in the exergue (below the main design)
  • Often accompanied by mintmark ML (Londinium) or C (Camulodunum)
  • Most common on silver denarii, but also on some antoniniani
  • Quality varies ‚Äî look for clear legends and good silver content

Historical Context

Carausius declared himself Emperor of Britain and Northern Gaul in 286 AD. For seven years, he successfully defended his breakaway state against Rome:

286 AD
Carausius seizes power, opens mint at Londinium
289 AD
Defeats Maximian's invasion fleet
290 AD
Secures temporary recognition as co-emperor
293 AD
Assassinated by his finance minister Allectus
296 AD
Allectus falls to Constantius Chlorus; Britain returns to Rome

After Allectus fell, Rome attempted damnatio memoriae ‚Äî erasing both usurpers from history. They removed coins from circulation, overturned milestones, and never spoke their names in official speeches.

Yet the coins survived. And 1,700 years later, we can still read Virgil in the exergue of a Londinium denarius.
